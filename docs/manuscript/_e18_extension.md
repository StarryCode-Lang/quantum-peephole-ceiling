# E18 Survivorship Bias: Extended Discussion

> **Context**: Extension of Section 6.3.5 (Clifford+T Decomposition Overhead) in `manuscript.md`. This file supplements, and does not modify, the main manuscript.

## The Ceiling for Clifford+T Circuits Is Conditional on Successful Decomposition

Experiment E18 evaluates Clifford+T gate-set decomposition across 270 attempted circuit rows, but the headline statistics rest on a filtered subset. Of those 270 rows, 78 produce `NaN` fidelity at the decomposition stage (the Clifford+T encoding routine cannot represent parameterized rotations within the allocated precision budget), and an additional 42 rows pass decomposition but yield `fidelity = 0` (the resulting circuit is not unitarily equivalent to the input, indicating a numerical or structural defect in the decomposition pipeline). Together, 120 of 270 rows (44.4%) are excluded before any optimization analysis begins. The 150 surviving rows carry a mean fidelity of 0.7812 when computed over all non-NaN entries — a figure that itself is depressed by the 42 zero-fidelity rows and rises to approximately 1.0 on the strictly valid subset. This filtering is not random: it enriches the survivor set for circuits whose gate composition is amenable to Clifford+T decomposition (fewer parameterized rotations, fewer non-Clifford multi-qubit gates), meaning that reduction rates and ceiling classifications computed on the survivors overstate the optimism of the result for the general circuit population.

The 44.4% failure rate demonstrates that Clifford+T decomposition is not universally applicable. Toffoli gates require 7 T gates and 6 CNOT gates in the Clifford+T basis, and parameterized rotations require $O(\log(1/\varepsilon))$ T gates for precision $\varepsilon$. For circuit families rich in these gate types — which include several of the 15 families in our benchmark — the decomposition overhead overwhelms any subsequent optimization benefit. This is a known limitation of the project, registered as L5 in the README's Known Limitations table (severity: Medium). The failure is not a limitation of the structural-ceiling framework itself, which is gate-set-specific by construction: the ceiling for a given family may differ under different gate sets, and the qualitative trichotomy (Regime I, II, III) may shift as gates are decomposed. Rather, it is a limitation of the Clifford+T decomposition pipeline as an optimization benchmarking tool.

Consequently, the E18 results should not be generalized to the failed rows or to the full attempted population. The ceiling for Clifford+T circuits is conditional on successful decomposition: it characterizes the optimization landscape only for those circuits that survive the encoding step. The surviving rows represent a best-case subset, and the mean reduction observed on them should be interpreted as an upper bound on the population mean, not a representative estimate. A rerun on the full 270-row ensemble — contingent on fixing the decomposition pipeline defect responsible for the 42 fidelity-zero rows — is scheduled as future work (Section 15.13 of the Limitations chapter). Until that rerun is completed, all E18 conclusions in the manuscript carry the "survivorship-biased" qualifier at first mention, and readers should weight them accordingly.
